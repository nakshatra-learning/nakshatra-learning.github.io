Lesson 2

Project setup & organization

Every Altium design starts with a PCB Project (.PrjPcb). This container holds your schematics, PCB files, libraries, output jobs and version control settings.

  1. File → New → Project → PCB Project
  2. Save it with a meaningful name (e.g. Multivibrator.PrjPcb) inside its own folder.
  3. Create subfolders inside: CODE, DATASHEETS, Libraries.
  4. Right-click the project → Add New to Project → Schematic.

Lesson 3

Schematic capture — Active Bar & Properties

When you open a blank schematic, Altium switches to a context-aware Active Bar with placement controls for components, nets, buses, sheet symbols and more.

  • Open the Libraries panel — search e.g. 2N3904 in Miscellaneous Devices.IntLib, click Place.
  • Press Tab while placing to open the Properties panel (set Designator like Q1).
  • Manipulate floating part: X = flip horizontal, Y = flip vertical, Space = rotate 90° CCW.
  • Connect with the Wire tool. Use Net Labels (VCC, GND) to merge nets across sheets without drawing wires.
  • Run Project → Compile PCB Project to validate the schematic before pushing to PCB.
Tip: dock the Properties panel to the left side — you'll be using it constantly to edit Designator, Value, footprint and other parameters.
Lesson 4

Design rules — the heart of the PCB editor

Every PCB constraint in Altium lives inside the Rules & Constraints Editor (Design → Rules). Rules are evaluated by priority and checked continuously while you route.

Electrical

Clearance, short-circuit detection, un-routed nets, un-connected pins.

Routing

Trace width, topology, routing layers, via styles, fanout control.

Manufacturing

Hole size, annular ring, minimum solder mask sliver, silkscreen-to-pad clearance.

High-Speed

Length matching, parallelism, differential pair spacing, stub length limits.

Use the Design Rule Wizard to auto-generate rules for net classes — e.g. all USB diff-pairs get a 90Ω matched-length constraint.

Note: rules are hierarchical. A narrower scope overrides a broader one. Understand rule priority before writing complex nets.
Lesson 5

Interactive routing & 3D view

After Design → Update PCB Document pushes the schematic into a board file, you can place components and route. Altium's Interactive Router shoves obstacles, pushes vias, hugs and walks around them in real time.

  • Single-net routing — straightforward signal trace routing.
  • Multi-net routing — route a bus while honoring spacing rules between all signals.
  • Differential pair routing — maintains gap and length-match for USB/PCIe/LVDS pairs.
  • Auto-router — useful for low-priority nets, but never rely on it for power, clocks or high-speed.

Press 3 in the PCB editor to switch to 3D View — verify mechanical fit, connector alignment and component collisions before fabrication.

Lesson 6

How a PCB is actually manufactured

Understanding the manufacturing flow separates a good designer from a great one. A typical multilayer PCB production process:

01
Data Prep

Convert Gerbers/IPC-2581 to tooling — panelization & drill programs.

02
Inner-Layer

Apply dry-film resist, UV expose through artwork, develop and etch copper.

03
Lamination

Bond cores, prepreg and copper foil under heat & pressure.

04
Drill & Plate

Mechanical drilling, deburr, desmear, electroless & electroplate copper.

05
Outer Layer

Photoresist, expose, plate copper + tin, etch, strip resist.

06
Solder Mask

Liquid photoimageable mask applied, exposed, developed and cured.

07
Surface Finish

HASL, ENIG or OSP protects exposed copper for soldering.

08
Legend & Route

Silkscreen, then board profiling, scoring, slots and chamfers.

Lesson 7

Design for Manufacturing (DFM) — get it right the first time

DFM is the discipline of designing a PCB that is manufacturable, functional and reliable. Done well, it eliminates respins, reduces revision time and gets products to market faster.

Goals when applying DFM:

  • Eliminate respins caused by manufacturing-specific details missed in design.
  • Produce boards that are both manufacturable and functional on the first build.
  • Hit time-to-market targets consistently by following a known set of best practices.
  • Pick materials suited to your environment (FR-4, high-Tg, polyimide, RF substrates).
  • Plan the stackup early — don't treat it as an afterthought.
  • Place components with assembly clearance & orientation in mind.
  • Define test points so the fab can electrically verify your board.
  • Document fully — fab notes, master drawing, assembly drawing.

Lesson 8

Selecting materials & stackup

The base material affects mechanical strength, cost, signal integrity, thermal performance and high-frequency loss. Choose based on your application:

Standard FR-4

Woven glass + epoxy. Cheap, reliable, good enough for most consumer designs up to ~1 GHz.

High-Tg FR-4

Tg ≥ 170°C. Better for lead-free assembly and dense multilayer boards with long thermal cycles.

Polyimide

Extreme heat tolerance, excellent flexibility — the standard for flex and rigid-flex boards.

RF Substrates (Rogers)

Low-loss, controlled Dk/Df for high-frequency RF and high-speed digital designs above 1 GHz.

Lesson 9

Component placement & orientation

Good placement makes routing easy and assembly cheap. Rules of thumb for professional-grade layouts:

  • Group by function: power section, MCU + decoupling caps, analog front-end, connectors at the board edge.
  • Decouplers go close: place 100 nF caps within ~2 mm of the IC power pin they decouple.
  • Crystals near the MCU: short, symmetric traces with a ground guard ring underneath.
  • Connectors at the edge: mating, strain relief and cable routing all benefit from edge placement.
  • Orient SMD passives consistently: all 0805s aligned in the same direction speeds pick-and-place and improves wave-soldering yield.

Lesson 10

Vias & holes — clearance, sizing and tenting

Vias transmit electrical current between layers and can be a significant burden on manufacturing costs if clearance and sizing guidelines aren't followed.

  • Standard vias — maintain minimum clearances from adjacent conductors. Clearance depends on whether the via is tented or exposed.
  • Via size — maintain a 1:1 aspect ratio between hole diameter and substrate thickness (e.g. 0.20″ holes in 0.20″ substrate).
  • Tented vias — via hole covered with solder mask. Default method; protects against bridging especially near BGA pads.
  • Exposed vias — require 0.15″ minimum clearance (0.20″ preferred) to adjacent conductors.

Lesson 11

Trace routing — connecting pads the right way

Every pad should be connected to its own trace, routing from outside or inside the edges of the pad while keeping routing symmetrical — critical in areas without soldermask to prevent solder migration during assembly.

  • Necking a trace: where a trace connects to a component pad, keep the neck no wider than 0.010″ for at least 0.010″ before expanding to the full trace width.
  • U-configuration for leaded SMD: route over and back in a flipped "U" rather than directly between lands in an "H" shape.
  • Planes and traces: keep power/ground planes on internal layers, symmetrical and centered to prevent board warping.
  • Plating options: HASL, ENIG, OSP — consult your manufacturer for which finish best matches your assembly process.

Lesson 12

Rigid-flex design — when bending matters

Rigid-flex combines rigid FR-4 sections with flexible polyimide sections in a single board — eliminating connectors and cables, saving space, and surviving repeated mechanical movement.

Substrate

Polyimide film (1–3 mil) is the standard — tough, very flexible and heat resistant.

Conductors

Use Rolled Annealed (RA) copper for repeated bending. ED copper is fine for static flex sections.

Coverlay

Polyimide film (or flexible solder mask) protects the outer flex conductors from damage.

Adhesives

Acrylic or epoxy bond the layers — beware "squeeze-out" into the bend area during lamination.

Do's & Don'ts for flex routing:

  • Do use curved traces in bend regions — never sharp 90° corners.
  • Do add teardrops or anchor pads where traces enter/exit pads.
  • Do use cross-hatched copper fills on flex layers — solid pours destroy flexibility.
  • Don't place vias in or near the bend zone.
  • Don't change trace width abruptly — taper gradually instead.
  • Don't bend at the corner of a rigid section — leave a bend relief area.
